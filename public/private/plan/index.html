The drill log: one file, five categories, two retests

The plan says "mistake log" five times; here is what it actually is. One file, drill-log.md, in the jetson-lab repo — greppable, versioned, and visible to Claude. One line per miss, written within 60 seconds of the miss:

07-08 · GB 2.14 · CONCEPT · treated the draws as independent
        fix: write the sample space before touching arithmetic · retest ☐ +48h ☐ FRI
07-09 · LC 239 · BLANK · never seen the monotonic-deque idea
        fix: Atlas Lab 17, then 3 more sliding-window mediums · retest ☐ +48h ☐ FRI
CategoryWhat it meansWhat it prescribes
MISREADAnswered a different question than the one askedA ritual, not more math: restate the ask in one line before solving
CONCEPTThe idea itself was missing or wrongSTUDY time (Atlas, notes) — never "more reps"; reps rehearse the error
MECHRight idea, arithmetic or index slipMore mental-math reps + a slower final pass
SPEEDGot it, too lateReps of that exact pattern until it's boring
BLANKNo foothold at allThe pattern isn't learned yet — it moves up the ladder, this week
  • A miss isn't logged until the fix is one actionable sentence. "Be more careful" is not a fix; "restate the ask before solving" is.
  • Two retests per miss: at +48h, and at the Friday session. Fail both → it becomes a flashcard plus a named 15-min STUDY slot next week. No third silent failure.
  • Friday counts categories, not problems. The modal category is the instruction: mostly MISREAD → slow the reading ritual; mostly SPEED → more reps; mostly CONCEPT → more STUDY; mostly BLANK → the ladder is moving too fast, say so at review.
  • This file is two SHIPs. Week 7's STUDY is reading it cover to cover; week 8's SHIP (mistake-log v2) is this same file with every line categorized and fixed — already in the plan, now with a format.

05 · Accountability

The mechanics that beat idle reading

  • The artifact rule. Reading and watching count for exactly zero. A week happened only if its SHIP box is checked, and a SHIP is always something another person could look at: a repo, a table of numbers, a posted note, applications submitted. This is the whole anti-idle-reading mechanism — you can't passively consume your way to a checked SHIP box.
  • Friday review, 20 minutes. Three questions, written where Claude can see them: What shipped? What stalled and why (be specific — "tired" is allowed, "busy" is not, name what ate the time)? What's the ONE build for next week?
  • The two-miss rule. Miss two SHIPs in a row → the plan is wrong, not you. Shrink scope with Claude (drop STUDY, halve BUILD) rather than silently abandoning the whole thing. Plans die of shame, not of difficulty.
  • Drills are exempt from negotiation from now until your last interview. 15–30 min/day. Recall is a muscle with a ~2-week half-life.
  • Want a robot? Ask Claude to "set up a weekly Friday check-in for the training plan" — a scheduled agent can pull this page's state, ask the three questions, and nag you about unchecked SHIPs. Say the word and it exists.

The ADHD operating layer

Everything above assumes the hours exist. This section makes them exist. Design around three facts instead of fighting them: starting is the hard part (not capacity), unstructured time evaporates, and novelty is fuel, not the enemy.

WEEK 0 — SET UP THE MACHINE (once, this weekend)

RULES OF ENGAGEMENT

  • Blocks are 45–90 minutes with a visible timer, and you stop while it's still interesting. Parking on a downhill slope makes tomorrow's start free — ending at the wall makes it dreadful.
  • The productive pivot. When a BUILD stalls and you feel the drift starting, you may swap to any other box on this page — a drill set, a C++ rep, the writeup — but not to the phone. This harnesses novelty-seeking instead of moralizing about it. The plan has enough variety on purpose.
  • Streaks are banned. A missed day is information, not debt. Guilt is the actual killer of ADHD systems — it converts one missed session into an abandoned plan. The two-miss rule exists so the plan bends instead of breaking.
  • Use the post-gym window. The 30–60 minutes after training is the cheapest focus you'll ever get — put drills or a short study block there on gym days.
  • The noticing ritual. Caught yourself scrolling mid-block? Stand up, park the phone, read NEXT.md, sit down. No self-flagellation step — it's not in the ritual, so don't add it.
  • Protect sleep like it's load-bearing, because it is. Regular sleep is the single biggest consistency lever there is. If the phone sleeps in your bedroom, a $10 alarm clock fixes more than any productivity app.

06 · Philosophy

“Learn it once, then let AI do it” — mostly right, two edits

Your instinct is correct and it's how the next decade works: manual competence first (so you can specify, verify, and debug what AI produces), then delegation, then move up a level. Do-it-once is the price of being able to judge AI output — you can't review a CUDA kernel you couldn't have written slowly yourself. But it needs two amendments:

Moat skills

Overlearn, never delegate. GPU/systems intuition is your differentiation — the thing you're better at than the model's median user. Depth compounds here; "once" is not enough. This is the Jetson ladder.

Interview skills

Drill on a schedule. Interviews test unaided recall at speed. "I did green book problems once in July" fails an October superday. Spaced repetition until the offer, then let it fade guilt-free.

Everything else

Your rule, verbatim. Do it once for the mental model (one linker, one compiler pass, one backtest), then delegate to AI forever and spend the freed hours on the first two columns.

One more poke: "learn once" has a failure mode you're prone to — collecting first times the way some people collect tabs. Fourteen threads is what that looks like. The fix is already on this page: three threads, twelve SHIP boxes.
